Semantic text tagging

A short example

The core is the semantic tagging of the texts. Let's illustrate with a simple contrived example. The text is:

open windows

Both words have more than one meaning. The verb "open" can be one of many forms of "to open", like for example the imperative, or an adjective. The predicate "windows" can refer to a glassed aperture in the wall or to an operating system.

Translating this piece of text into Spanish could yield "abre ventanas", or "ventanas abiertas", or "inicia Windows".

We have an opportunity to be amused by those weird automatic translations.

If the text was semantically tagged it wouldn't happen that easy. For example:

<semanticInfo="imperative of to open">open</semanticInfo> <semanticInfo="noun: a framework of wood or metal that contains a glass windowpane and is built into a wall or roof to admit light or air, plural">windows</semanticInfo>

This would severely constrain the chances of the translator program to do funny things.

In addition to tag the meaning of ambiguous words, we souls especially tag idioms and phrasal verbs, sayings that depart from the literal meaning of the words.

Little he knew ...

This saying, on which the film Stranger than reality pivots, is an example of a phrase needing cautious translation. Some literal translations wouldn't work. In Spanish there is no equivalent idiom, just the plain "Él no sospechaba que ...", it's not legal to invert the order of the parts in this way.

The semantic information for this phase is that a third person is informing the reader that something unexpected is about to happen, or happened, to an unaware subject.

Shakespeare knew only a little Latin, but he put the little he knew to very good use

The sentence above contains the same exact words but with a completely different meaning.

Little we know, as of today, about what could be the outcome of this text tagging thing.

What are the benefits?

A single translation for all languages

Tagged texts can be automatically translated to any language yielding non-embarrasing results. Of course, by means of a modified translator program, which is assumed to exist for now.

A text translated from English to tagged English could be used as a source for any other language.

A companion for readers

Anyone reading a tagged text and seeking deeper understanding can query it to be explained its meaning.

Be it a person reading the news or classic literature; in their mother language or a foreign one. Reading for the sake of reading, or while studying the language.

We have online dictionaries that can provide us all the meanings of a word; this will provide the exact meaning intended for the current instance of the word.

Who would do the text tagging?

Professional test taggers

A new profession will be born, somewhere in between copy proofing and literary critic.

Lawyers and poets

In the future the text of an international contract will need to be tagged in order to avoid "lost in translation" issues. The meaning constrains will be valuable, and the tagging of legal copy will be done by law and domain experts.

Poets will say that the tagging of texts doesn't leave much for the imagination, and in some cases this will be the desired outcome.

English students and teachers (English or any other language whatsoever)

One of the most boring tasks in the works is to prepare exam tests.

Text tagging comes to the rescue, providing language teachers an endless source of fresh excercises.

Depending on the level, the student can be posed two different types of tests. The simple ones show a word in the context of a paragraph and the student has to choose its meaning from a multiple choices list. A slightly higher level student sees not a word but an idiom (like a phrasal verb) and chooses the meaning. The teacher grades the answer.

For the higher level test the student is given a paragraph and has to identify the aception of the selected words and also identify all the idioms present in the copy and choose their semantics.

In all cases, the student can choose an "other" option when there is none that fits.

Gamers

Gamers "taming the translator" earning points by providing semantic hints to a piece of text until the translator can do a decent (or excellent) job
