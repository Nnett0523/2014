Semantic text tagging

A short example

The core is the semantic tagging of the texts, a means to overcome the current (and by sure enduring) lack of common sense of the computers. Let's illustrate with a simple contrived example.

open windows

In the text above both words have more than one meaning. The word "open" can be one of the many forms of "to open" like for example the imperative, or an adjective. The predicate "windows" can refer to a glassed aperture in the wall, a time lapse, or a computer operating system.

Translating this piece of text into Spanish could yield "abre ventanas" (do open windows), or "ventanas abiertas" (opened windows) , or "inicia Windows" (do start Windows).

If the text was semantically tagged the translation would be more precise. For example:

<p lang="en"><semanticInfo="imperative of to open">open</semanticInfo> <semanticInfo="noun: an opening, usually covered by one or more panes of clear glass, to allow light and air from outside to enter a building or vehicle">windows</semanticInfo></p>

This would severely constrain the chances of the translator program to do funny things.

In addition to tag the meaning of ambiguous words, we should especially tag idioms, proverbs and phrasal verbs, sayings that depart from the literal meaning of the words.

Little he knew ...

This saying, on which the film Stranger than reality pivots, is an example of a phrase needing cautious translation. Some literal translations wouldn't work.

The semantic information for this phrase is that a third person is informing the reader that something unexpected is about to happen, or happened, to an unaware subject.

Shakespeare knew only a little Latin, but he put the little he knew to very good use

The sentence above contains the same exact words but with a completely different meaning.

Little we know, as of today, about what could be the outcome of this text tagging thing, maybe a huge impact in the textual communitations through computers between different cultures.

What are would be benefits?

A single translation for all languages

Tagged texts can be automatically translated to any language yielding non-embarrasing results by means of a modified translator program, which is assumed to exist for now (this has still to be verified).

A text translated from English to tagged English could be used as a source for any other language.

Much better search results

There will be two results levels: the new semantic and the current lexical.

Semantic search results are matches of one selected meaning of a word against that same meaning found in tagged texts.

For example one that is planning the building of a house can search for "windows" as apertures and filter out all the humongouns set of windows-as-an-OS matches.

All the windows makers that publish web sites in English will want to be in the semantic set of search results.

After a while, usability tests will demonstrate that search users almost don't go beyond the semantic set, because of the extra effort needed to filter the results.

A companion for readers

Anyone reading a tagged text and seeking deeper understanding can query it to be explained its meaning.

Be it a person reading the news or classic literature; in their mother language or a foreign one. Reading for the sake of reading, or while studying the language.

We have online dictionaries that can provide us all the meanings of a word; this will provide the exact meaning intended for the current instance of the word. Not a listing of possible meanings but the current one.

Who would do the text tagging?

Professional test taggers

A new profession will be born, somewhere in between copy proofing and literary critic.

Lawyers and poets

In the future the text of an international contract will need to be tagged in order to avoid "lost in translation" issues. The meaning constrains will be valuable, and the tagging of legal copy will be done by law and domain experts.

Poets will say that the tagging of texts doesn't leave much for the imagination, and in some cases this will be the desired outcome.

English students and teachers (English or any other language whatsoever)

One of the most boring tasks in the whole world is to prepare exam tests.

Text tagging comes to the rescue, providing language teachers an endless source of fresh excercises.

Depending on the level, the student can be posed two different types of tests. The simple ones show a word in the context of a paragraph and the student has to choose its meaning from a multiple choices list. A slightly higher level student sees not a word but an idiom (like a phrasal verb) and chooses the meaning. The teacher U OIUYIYIUYNOIYOIUYNIYNOIUNNINinuyi uyiuyniu yn<oiuyniuynoiuyniy

In all cases, the student can choose an "other" option when there is none that fits.

Gamers

Gamers of "Taming the translator", earning points by adding semantic hints to a piece of text until the translator can do a decent (or excellent) job.

Growth

The dictionary

Tagging each word with its complete description is impractical. The web-right way is to relate each word with a set of URIs pointing to each word meaning, after some stemming to take plural or gender inflections.

The tag for the word "windows" should be something similar to:

<p lang="en"><semanticInfo="http://en.adictionary.net/window?1">

The tag URI refers to the first entry ("?1") of the "window" entry from the English version of the shared dictionary.

It points to content similar to the "window" entry in Wiktionary, that looks like:

  1. An opening, usually covered by one or more panes of clear glass, to allow light and air from outside to enter a building or vehicle.
  2. An opening, usually covered by glass, in a shop which allows people to view the shop and its products from outside.
  3. (architecture) The shutter, casement, sash with its fittings, or other framework, which closes a window opening.
  4. A period of time when something is available.
    launch window;  window of opportunity
    You have a two-hour window of clear weather to finish working on the lawn.
  5. (graphical user interface) A rectangular area on a computer terminal or screen containing some kind of user interface, displaying the output of and allowing input for one of a number of simultaneously running computer processes.
  6. A figure formed of lines crossing each other.
  7. (added) An operating system for personal computers.

Actually most of the URI can be built from information in the page: the language is declared in general, the dictionary domain is known data, the word id part of the text. The only additional information needed is the ordinal number that point to the specific word meaning and, if we agreed that if it's missing then the first one is the choice then the URI vanishes in full.

Not a single dictionary

The process of tagging great quantities of text should yield also an ever-evolving dictionary.

Having a single dictionary for all sounds somehow Orwellian. Actually, people should have freedom to point to any dictionary of their choice.

This is a business opportunity, where businesses can build their own dictionaries as services. For example LexisNexis could offer a specialized dictionary fine tuned for legal copy, cascading into the general dictionary for terms not found in theirs.

As another example, there is the Department of Defense Dictionary of Military and Associated Terms that contains a few words to be used whre appropriate.

Not only words in the dictionary

The meaning of each word is important, but there are also that sequences of words that, in the appropriate context, acquire different meanings.

For example in "these new products provide more bang for the buck" or "this is a pain in the ass".

The semantic tagging not only tags words but also prases that are to be taken as idions and need special consideration for their translation.

In most cases it might be possible to provide, within the idioms dictionary information, a piece of replacement text that means more or less the same as the idiom but can stand a straighforward translation.

Moreover, many idioms and proverbs have equivalents in other languages that should be the replacement text after adapting number and gender where needed. For example "The early bird gets the worm" is "Al que madruga Dios lo ayuda", completely different but conveying the same meaning.
